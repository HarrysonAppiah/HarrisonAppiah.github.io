# Monday Musings

## *Life for Rent: The Illusion of Ownership in a Borrowed World*

There is something haunting about Life for Rent by Dido.
Not just the melody, but the quiet honesty hidden beneath it.

> “If my life is for rent, and I don’t learn to buy,
> Well I deserve nothing more than I get…”

For years, we are taught that life follows a reliable formula:
study hard, sacrifice sleep, collect degrees, build experience, survive interviews, and eventually arrive at stability. We are told education is an investment — that knowledge guarantees security and hard work guarantees dignity.

Then reality interrupts the script.
<!--more-->

You graduate into a job market demanding *entry-level candidates with five years of experience*. Companies advertise “competitive salaries” that barely compete with rent, groceries, or peace of mind. Positions require mastery of ten different skills, availability beyond working hours, emotional resilience, leadership experience, technical certifications, and somehow still offer compensation that feels like survival rather than reward.

It makes you wonder: what exactly are we working toward?

The modern economy has mastered the art of temporary ownership.
We lease apartments we may never truly afford.
We finance cars that belong more to banks than to us.
We build careers that can disappear after one corporate restructuring email.
Even time no longer feels personal; it is traded for productivity metrics, deadlines, and performance reviews.

And perhaps that is why *Life for Rent* feels timeless.

Because beneath the frustration of employment statistics and shrinking opportunities lies a deeper discomfort: the realization that many of the things we fight hardest to obtain were never fully ours to begin with.

Not titles.
Not institutions.
Not salaries.
Not certainty.

A degree once symbolized arrival. Today, for many people, it simply grants access to another waiting room.

Yet this is not entirely a pessimistic reflection.

There is strange freedom in recognizing impermanence. If life itself is temporary stewardship rather than ownership, then perhaps value should no longer be measured solely by job titles, income brackets, or professional validation.

Maybe fulfillment is found elsewhere:
in music that understands us,
in conversations that linger,
in communities that remember our names,
in curiosity, art, culture, and the courage to remain human in systems that constantly reduce people to output.

Anthropologists often argue that societies reveal themselves through what they reward. Ours rewards exhaustion remarkably well. But human beings were never designed to live only as economic instruments.

We are storytellers.
Meaning-makers.
Memory-carriers.

And maybe that is why songs survive longer than résumés.

This Monday, perhaps the question is not whether we own enough, but whether we are allowing the systems around us to own too much of us.

— *Monday Musings*
